#cyberark-labs 

In CyberArk, we use **LDAP** (which stands for Lightweight Directory Access Protocol) to connect the "digital fortress" to your company’s **main phonebook**, which is usually called **Active Directory**.

Think of it like this: Instead of having to create a separate library card for every single student at a school, the library just connects its computer to the school’s main office list. This way, if you are a student at the school, the library already knows who you are!

Here is why we use it for components and users:

### 1. One Password for Everything (Single Sign-On)

Without LDAP, an admin like **Mike** would need one password to log into his computer, another for his email, and a _third_ special password just for CyberArk. By using LDAP, Mike can log into the **PVWA website** using the same credentials he uses for the rest of his job.

### 2. Automatic "Job" Assignments (Directory Mapping)

The company phonebook (LDAP) has everyone sorted into groups, like "Managers," "Auditors," or "IT Staff." CyberArk uses a trick called **Directory Mapping** to look at that phonebook and automatically give people the right "keys" based on their group.

- **Example:** If the phonebook says Cindy is an "Auditor," CyberArk automatically gives her the "Auditor" job inside the Vault without a human having to click any buttons.

### 3. Managing the Component Servers

The servers that run CyberArk (like **`comp01`**, which holds the PVWA and CPM tools) are usually Windows servers. In this lab, we use LDAP so that **Mike** can log into the Windows part of those servers using his company account.

- **Safety Note:** While the lab lets us do this for convenience, the guide warns that in the "real world," these sensitive component servers should actually have their own passwords hidden inside the Vault for extra safety!

### 4. Handling Domain Accounts

Some CyberArk components need to go out and "scout" the network to find other computers. For example, the **Discovery Account (`cpm-scan`)** is an LDAP/Active Directory account. Because it is a domain account, it has the power to look at many different computers in the "Acme Corp" network to see if they are safe.

### Key Terms to Remember:

- **Active Directory (AD):** The actual "phonebook" file where the company keeps its list of employees.
- **LDAP:** The "language" CyberArk uses to talk to that phonebook.
- **Transparent Users:** These are the people (like Dexter or Cindy) who exist in the company phonebook and can just "show up" and log into CyberArk without being manually created there first.
